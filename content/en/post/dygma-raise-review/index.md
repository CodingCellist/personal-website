The Dygma Raise is an ergonomic, split mechanical keyboard which I stumbled
across about 2 years ago, when I was looking at getting a more comfortable
working from home (WFH) setup. If you're wondering why I chose this keyboard
specifically, there's a more in-depth discussion of my considerations
[here](/en/post/my-ergonomic-wfh-setup#picking-an-ergonomic-keyboard),
but the tl;dr is: It was a mechanical keyboard which had a model with Nordic ISO
layout and backlighting; based on
[their YouTube videos](https://youtu.be/EoNkMBY7qKI),
the thumb buttons seemed well thought-through; and the hardware, firmware, and
config software being open-source is something I find cool and wanted to
support.

At the time of writing, I have been using the Dygma Raise for about a year and a
half. I use it pretty much daily for about 8-10 hours, albeit less on the
weekends, where I prefer to see the outside (strange, I know).

Anyway, on to the review.


### Setting up and OOTB experience

Spending ~€300 on a keyboard, you really don't want to feel like you wasted the
money. Or rather, you'd like the product to feel like it was worth €300; if it
arrives in a cheap plastic case and weighs 200g, you'd probably feel
disappointed.

Thankfully, the Raise does neither: It arrives in a nice carrying case, and has
a comforting heft to it. It both feels and looks premium. This being my first
split keyboard, of course the first thing I wanted to try was to split it! This
too feels nice and reliable: The amount of force required to separate the halves
is not scary, but it also doesn't feel like they'll just separate by themselves.
Putting it back together took a bit of getting used to (there are 4 rods and
cylinders that slot together), but again, it feels reliable and like it's
designed to last. Over a year later, and I'm happy that there has been no
loosening of the rods; it still feels as solidly held together as the day I got
it.

Setting it up is also nice and simple: Connect the two halves to the central hub
(the "neuron", as it's called), then plug that into your computer and voilà! You
now have a working, split, QWERTY keyboard and can get back to typing without
any need for additional configuration. However, as you type, you probably notice
that you'd like access to things like `Delete`, `Home`, `End`, arrow keys,
function keys, etc. Which means you'll want "layers". This may sound like a new
thing, but if you've been using computers with a physical keyboard, you've
actually used them all along!


### Layer configuration

To configure the Raise, you need to download the "Bazecor" software. It's an
Electron app (I know, I know) which allows you to modify the key-mapping, define
layers, and update the firmware of the keyboard. Downloading and installing
Bazecor thankfully also seems to be a smooth process: there is an `.exe` for
Windows, an `AppImage` for Linux, and a `.dmg` (whatever that is) for MacOS.

Now, remember how I said you've been using layers all your (computer-aware)
life? I was talking about keys like `Shift` and `CapsLock`. These change what
keys on your keyboard do what, i.e. they change what _layer_ you're using. On a
60% keyboard like the Raise, you only get the alphanumerical keys and the
modifiers around them. There are no function keys, no arrow keys, no media keys
(volume up/down, play/pause), no `Home` or `PgUp` like you might be used to on
your laptop or desktop keyboard.


### And we're off!

With some basic layers configured, you can now begin using the Raise to its full
potential.


### A year without tenting

If you thought this was anything other than the angle between the keyboard and
the table, get your mind out of the gutter!

The Raise keyboard initially came as just a split keyboard; there were no
accessories to adjust the angle of the keyboard relative to the table. After
numerous requests from the community, however, Dygma released the "Raise Tenting
Kit": A set of plates which could be screwed onto the bottom of the keyboard and
allowed it to be tented at angles of 10°, 20°, 30°, and 40°.

I was initially sceptical as to whether tenting made a difference. Having used
"flat"/normal keyboard orientations my entire life, I suspected the main
difference was getting the split keyboard (since that aligned my keyboard with
my shoulder-width) and that tenting might be too weird or uncomfortable.
Nevertheless, I ended up taking a similar approach to tenting as I originally
did with learning Vim: a lot of people seem to like this, so there must be
something about it, and  ordered a white kit to match my keyboard. I did also
come across the term "wrist pronation" in the
[ShortCircuit video](https://youtu.be/i4TCbc9oB1E?t=298) discussing the Raise,
and (looking further into this) it seemed to be a real issue and one which
[several](https://ultimatehackingkeyboard.com/knowledgebase/can-i-tent-or-tilt-the-uhk)
[ergonomic](https://kinesis-ergo.com/solutions/keyboard-risk-factors/#forearm-pronation)
[keyboards](https://ergodox-ez.com/products/tilt-tent-kit?variant=16101844419),
[including](https://www.microsoft.com/en-gb/d/microsoft-ergonomic-keyboard/93841ngdwr1h)
[off-the-shelf](https://www.microsoft.com/en-gb/d/microsoft-sculpt-ergonomic-desktop/8xk02kz6k69w)
[ones](https://www.logitech.com/en-gb/products/keyboards/k860-split-ergonomic.html)

### Summary

- **Feel+OOTB experience:** Great.
- **Challenges:** If you're not used to touch-typing/10-finger typing, you're
    going to have a rough time. If you already know how to touch type, the main
    thing is retraining your muscle memory to use your thumbs more and to
